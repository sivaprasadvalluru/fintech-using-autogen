Value investing involves buying securities that appear underpriced by some form of fundamental analysis. Value investors actively seek stocks of companies that they believe the market has undervalued.
Growth investing focuses on companies expected to grow at an above-average rate compared to other companies. Growth investors often look at the company's potential for future earnings increases.
Income investing focuses on investments that generate a regular income, such as bonds, dividend-paying stocks, and real estate investment trusts (REITs).
Dollar-cost averaging is the practice of investing a fixed amount of money in a particular investment on a regular schedule, regardless of the share price. Over time, this strategy can reduce the impact of volatility.
Index investing aims to replicate the performance of a specific index, such as the S&P 500. This passive strategy offers lower management fees and has historically outperformed many actively managed funds.
Momentum investing involves buying securities that have had high returns over a previous period and selling those that have had poor returns. This strategy is based on the idea that securities that have performed well in the past will continue to perform well in the short term.
Contrarian investing involves going against prevailing market trends by buying assets that are performing poorly and selling when they perform well.
ESG investing considers environmental, social, and governance factors in investment decisions, aiming for both financial return and positive societal impact.
Swing trading is a style of trading that attempts to capture short- to medium-term gains in a stock (or any financial instrument) over a period of a few days to several weeks.
Pairs trading is a market-neutral strategy involving two correlated securities; when the relationship diverges, the investor buys the undervalued and sells the overvalued security.
Options strategies, such as covered calls and protective puts, are used to hedge risk or generate additional income in a portfolio.
Global macro investing is a strategy based on the interpretation and prediction of large-scale economic and political events to profit from macroeconomic trends.
Factor investing targets specific drivers of return across asset classes, such as value, size, momentum, and quality.
Tactical asset allocation is an active management portfolio strategy that shifts the percentage of assets held in various categories to take advantage of market pricing anomalies or strong market sectors.
