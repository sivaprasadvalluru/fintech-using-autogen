Fundamental analysis involves examining a company's financial statements and economic indicators to determine its intrinsic value. This approach focuses on factors like revenue, earnings, future growth, and return on equity.
Technical analysis involves evaluating securities based on market activity, such as past prices and volume. Technical analysts believe that historical price patterns tend to repeat themselves.
Sentiment analysis in market context evaluates investor attitudes and emotions toward specific securities or markets. This can be done through surveys, social media analysis, or monitoring news sentiment.
SWOT analysis examines a company's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats to evaluate its competitive position. This helps investors understand a company's strategic position in its market.
Porter's Five Forces model analyzes industry competition through five factors: competitive rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitution, and threat of new entry. It helps investors understand industry dynamics.
Economic cycle analysis involves examining where the economy is in its cycle (expansion, peak, contraction, trough) to make investment decisions. Different sectors often perform differently during various economic cycle phases.
Quantitative analysis uses mathematical and statistical modeling, measurement, and research to understand behavior and make investment decisions.
Elliott Wave Theory is a technical analysis approach that investors use to analyze financial market cycles and forecast market trends by identifying extremes in investor psychology.
Candlestick patterns are used in technical analysis to predict future price movements based on historical price data.
Volume analysis examines the number of shares or contracts traded in a security or market to confirm trends and chart patterns.
Backtesting is the process of testing a trading strategy on historical data to see how it would have performed.
Correlation analysis measures the relationship between two securities or asset classes to diversify or hedge a portfolio.
Market breadth indicators analyze the number of stocks advancing versus declining to gauge the overall direction of the market.
Sentiment indicators, such as the put/call ratio or volatility index (VIX), help investors assess market mood and potential turning points.
